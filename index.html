Frequently Asked Questions on the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples

The United Nations (UN) is the largest international organization, comprising of 193 Member States. It acts as a forum for international cooperation to support world peace. The UN was officially established on October 24, 1945 (now known as United Nations Day), after the end of the Second World War.

The UN’s operations are guided by the principles outlined in the UN Charter. They include maintaining international peace and security, developing friendly relations among nations, achieving international cooperation in solving international problems, and being a centre for harmonizing the actions of nations.

The UN Charter was signed on June 26, 1945, in San Francisco and serves as the cornerstone document for the organization. Under the UN Charter, there are six main bodies or organs that guide the UN: the General Assembly, the Security Council, the Economic and Social Council, the Trusteeship Council, the International Court of Justice, and the United Nations Secretariat.

In addition to the UN Charter, world leaders decided to create a document that would accompany the UN Charter and guarantee the rights of every individual everywhere. This document is the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which was adopted by the UN General Assembly on December 10, 1948, and contains 30 articles on human rights.

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which has been translated into over 500 languages, serves as a standard for evaluating ethical conduct and establishing a foundation for fair and equal treatment. It guarantees universal fundamental human rights for all and provides individuals worldwide with a powerful instrument in their struggle against oppression, injustice, and violations of human rights.

A declaration is formal statement or document issued by a government or an international organization promoting a common goal and expressing shared beliefs, principles, values, or commitments. Two notable examples of this are the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples.

In the UN, declarations are approved by the UN General Assembly but are not legally enforceable. This is because the UN General Assembly does not have the legislative powers to create international law. In other words, declarations are not law.

While declarations themselves may lack legal enforcement, they can influence the development of treaties or conventions, or lead to the enactment of new legislation (or the amendment of previous legislation) within a country or state.


The UN Declaration is a globally recognized comprehensive international human rights instrument that contains 46 articles on the collective and individual rights of Indigenous Peoples. Article 43 states that the rights of Indigenous Peoples in the UN Declaration are the minimum standards to contribute to their survival, dignity and well-being.

The UN Declaration is the result of almost 25 years of work and collaboration between UN member states and Indigenous Peoples from around the world. Indigenous leaders from Canada played a significant role in its development, including drafting and negotiations.

The UN Declaration was adopted by the UN General Assembly in 2007, although it wasn’t endorsed by the Government of Canada until May 2016.

Both the UN Declaration and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights are vital. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights applies universally to all people in the world, while the UN Declaration offers specific guidance to address the unique needs and rights of Indigenous Peoples.
The UN Declaration recognizes Indigenous Peoples’ unique historical and cultural contexts. It interprets and explains the human rights of Indigenous Peoples through an Indigenous lens and covers all areas of human rights as they relate to Indigenous communities, including:


  • Racism and discrimination: Addresses racism and discrimination, which involves acknowledging the inherent dignity and value of Indigenous Peoples and individuals.
  • Self-determination: Enables Indigenous communities to have autonomy in managing their own matters.
  • Participation in decision-making: Makes sure their voices are taken into account in issues that impact them.
  • Respect for and protection of culture: Safeguards Indigenous traditions, cultures, languages, and heritage.

The UN Declaration is a component of the broader UN human rights framework, which includes nine fundamental international human rights treaties. These treaties also safeguard and pertain to Indigenous Peoples. Collectively, these declarations advocate for justice, equality, and respect for all individuals.

The Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues is the primary body within the UN that addresses the realities of Indigenous Peoples. Established in 2000, the Forum held its first session in 2002. The Forum consists of 16 members, half selected by governments and the other half selected by Indigenous organizations.

The Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues provides expert guidance and recommendations on Indigenous realities to the Economic and Social Council and other UN programs. The Forum also strives to raise awareness and promote the coordination of activities related to Indigenous realities within the UN system.

Additionally, the Forum is responsible for organizing and distributing information on Indigenous realities and works to ensure that the provisions of the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples are respected and fully applied.

Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples

The Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples is an expert in the field of Indigenous rights who is appointed by the Commission on Human Rights. Their mandate is to promote the rights of Indigenous Peoples, report on their human rights situations, and address specific instances where Indigenous Peoples’ rights may have been violated.

The Special Rapporteur visits nations in order to report on the progress made by governments in supporting and respecting the rights of Indigenous Peoples.

Expert Mechanism on the Right of Indigenous Peoples

The Expert Mechanism on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples is a body of seven experts who are appointed by the Human Rights Council to provide advice to the UN Member States on achieving the objectives of the UN Declaration.

The group conducts studies and research on various issues, including self-determination, free and prior consent, education, and the right to participate in decision-making.

The UN Declaration addresses both the individual and collective rights of Indigenous Peoples, and establishes the critical standards for preserving Indigenous rights to contribute to their survival, dignity, and well-being.

Individual rights

There are other rights that apply to individuals, and there are rights that are both individual and collective. The right to security of the person, which includes the right to live free from violence, is a right that every individual has. But in the UN Declaration context, there is also a collective right to security, so this right is both individual and collective.

Collective rights

Indigenous Peoples have the collective right to live in freedom, peace and security as distinct peoples and the right not to be subjected to any act of genocide or any other act of violence, including forcibly removing children of the group to another group.

Implementing the UN Declaration is a significant step forward on the shared path of reconciliation. It will contribute to building renewed, nation-to-nation, Inuit-Crown, government-to-government relationships with First Nations, Inuit, and Métis based on affirmation of rights, respect, cooperation and partnership.

Implementing the UN Declaration is about:

  • creating a brighter future for present and future generations
  • continuing the journey of justice, peace and reconciliation
  • continuing to break down barriers and combat systemic racism and discrimination
  • affirming and recognizing the inherent rights of Indigenous Peoples, including the rights to self-determination and self-government
  • protecting Indigenous governance and laws, Indigenous lands and territories, and Indigenous cultures and languages
  • promoting economic participation and creating social and economic equality

Implementing the UN Declaration in Canada sets a positive example for upholding and respecting the human rights of Indigenous Peoples throughout the world.

Implementing the UN Declaration also responds to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s Call to Action 43, and the National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls Calls for Justice.

Long before the arrival of European and other settlers, Indigenous Peoples in what is now Canada had established their own unique forms of governance. These governance structures reflected the diverse economic, social, and geographic aspects of Indigenous communities, incorporating their cultural practices and spiritual beliefs. Initially, partnerships between colonial governments and Indigenous nations were established through treaties, trade, and military alliances. However, over the course of many centuries, these relationships deteriorated due to successive laws, policies, and decisions that adopted a colonial and paternalistic approach.

One significant example is the Indian Act, enacted in 1876, which continues to shape the governance of most First Nations in Canada today. The Indian Act imposed a colonial system of governance on First Nation communities, wherein authority rested with the federal minister, contributing to the erosion of Indigenous autonomy over time.

Canada recognizes that Indigenous Peoples have an inherent right of self-government guaranteed in section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982, and articles 3 and 4 of the UN Declaration.

Article 3

  • Indigenous peoples have the right to self-determination. By virtue of that right they freely determine their political status and freely pursue their economic, social and cultural development.

Article 4

  • Indigenous peoples, in exercising their right to self-determination, have the right to autonomy or self-government in matters relating to their internal and local affairs, as well as ways and means for financing their autonomous functions.

The meaning of self-determination differs among First Nations, Métis, Inuit, and Indigenous organizations, academics, and researchers. Although there is no universally accepted definition of Indigenous self-determination, certain overarching themes emerge when discussing the concept.

A central feature of self-determination is Indigenous Peoples regaining control over their own lands and resources. Instruments facilitating self-determination include comprehensive land claim agreements, self-government agreements, and First Nation land management agreements.

Additionally, there is an agreement on the importance of direct engagement and the duty to consult with Indigenous Peoples when activities have the potential to impact existing Indigenous or treaty rights. Furthermore, self-determination involves a commitment to acknowledge and promote Indigenous Peoples' jurisdiction or self-determination as a fundamental element of effective, credible, and responsible governance.

Through its 46 articles, the UN Declaration affirms and sets out a broad range of collective and individual rights. These rights represent the minimum standards for the survival, dignity and well-being of Indigenous Peoples around the world.

The process has moved slowly, but a fruitful dialogue has been established among states and Indigenous Peoples over the years. Issues of human rights, lands and resources have been the subject of intense debates.

Human rights: Human rights are the rights we all have by virtue of being human. They include things like the right to self-determination. The UN Declaration is an international human rights instrument that strives to articulate and protect the inherent rights of Indigenous Peoples.

Inherent rights: Prior to colonization, Indigenous Peoples exercised their sovereignty as nations with their own laws, governments, cultures, and economies. Lands and territories were owned collectively, with responsibilities to be caretakers of the land and to share it with plants and animals. Inherent rights are all of these rights that Indigenous Peoples have always had since time immemorial. These include collective rights to self-determination, to land and territory, and to culture.

Treaty rights: Treaties are agreements between nations, between Indigenous Peoples and Canada. Treaties are meant to uphold inherent rights. But not all treaties are the same, and treaties can have both positive and negative impacts on human rights. Treaty rights are recognized and affirmed in section 35 of the Constitution.

Constitutional rights: The Constitution is the supreme law of Canada. Section 35 of the Constitution recognizes and affirms existing Aboriginal and treaty rights, which are based on inherent rights. The Constitution does many things, including affirming human rights in Canadian law. It is a tool that impacts how human rights are respected, protected, and upheld in Canada. The Constitution can have positive and negative impacts on human rights.

When you are using the word “rights,” keep in mind which rights you are talking about. For the sake of clarity, it may help to say inherent rights, constitutional rights, treaty rights, or human rights instead of just “rights.”

On June 21, 2021, the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (the Act) became law. The Act affirmed the UN Declaration as an international human rights instrument that can help interpret and apply Canadian law. Under the Act, the Canadian government is required to collaborate with First Nations, Inuit and Métis to ensure that all legislation aligns with the principles outlined in the UN Declaration.

The Act provides a framework to advance implementation of the UN Declaration at the federal level, while also respecting Aboriginal and treaty rights affirmed in the Constitution. It ensures sustained efforts to uphold the human rights of Indigenous Peoples now and in the future, and contains measures to hold the federal government accountable.

The implementation of the Act continues the Government of Canada’s commitment to respond to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s Call to Action 44, which appeals to the Government of Canada “to develop a national action plan, strategies, and other concrete measures to achieve the goals” of the UN Declaration. Additionally, implementing the Act responds to Call for Justice 1.2 from the Final Report of the National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls.

The Act requires the Government of Canada, in consultation and cooperation with Indigenous Peoples, to:

  • take all measures necessary to ensure the laws of Canada are consistent with the UN Declaration
  • prepare and implement an action plan to achieve the UN Declaration’s objectives
  • table an annual report on the action plan and on progress aligning the laws of Canada

The Act plays a crucial role in the reconciliation process. This legislation recognizes the UN Declaration as an international human rights instrument that can aid in the interpretation and application of Canadian law. Through establishing the Act, Canada commits to implementing the UN Declaration at the federal level in collaboration with Indigenous Peoples

The implementation of the UN Declaration ensures accountability and provides clarity for the path ahead for Indigenous Peoples and all Canadians. It provides a legal framework that guarantees ongoing and persistent efforts to protect the human rights of Indigenous Peoples both currently and in the future.

The Government of Canada has a constitutional duty to consult Indigenous Peoples when it considers measures that might adversely impact their potential or established Aboriginal or treaty rights. This has been consistently confirmed by the courts. The duty to consult can also be set out in legislation or modern treaties. The Government of Canada has consistently worked to uphold this duty and has shown its commitment to taking additional steps to do so.

The implementation of the Act will inform how the government approaches meeting this legal duty going forward, providing greater clarity and creating greater certainty for Indigenous groups and all Canadians. However, the Act itself does not immediately change Canada’s existing duty to consult Indigenous groups, or other consultation and participation requirements set out in legislation like the Impact Assessment Act.

Article 19 of the UN Declaration declares that “States shall consult and cooperate in good faith with the indigenous peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent before adopting and implementing legislative or administrative measures that may affect them.”

  • Free: This implies that consent should be granted without any form of manipulation, coercion, or intimidation. Indigenous communities must have the autonomy to make decisions without external influence or pressure.

  • Prior: Prior to any authorization or commencement of activities, consent should be sought with sufficient advance notice. It is crucial to respect the time requirements of consultation and consensus processes.

  • Informed: Indigenous communities have the right to receive comprehensive information regarding any initiatives that impact their lands and resources. This includes projects related to minerals, water, the environment, and other resources. They should be informed in a timely manner and have the chance to give their consent before any development or action commences.

  • Consent: Consent refers to voluntarily and willfully agreeing to something in response to another person’s proposition. It involves giving assent or approval. When someone consents, they comply or yield to a request or proposal.

In everyday terms, the principle of free, prior, and informed consent dictates that governments and other entities cannot enact policies or programs concerning Indigenous Peoples’ lands unless they have consulted with and obtained consent from the Indigenous community. This principle is in alignment with Indigenous Peoples’ inherent right to self-determination. As the Principles Respecting the Government of Canada’s Relationship with Indigenous Peoples note, free, prior and informed consent builds on and goes beyond the legal duty to consult. For example, modern treaties have been described as an example of free, prior and informed consent in action.

Free, prior, and informed consent serves as a crucial safeguard, ensuring that Indigenous voices are acknowledged and respected in decisions that affect their lives and territories.

The Action Plan is a comprehensive plan encompassing 181 measures and overseen by more than 25 federal departments. It was shaped by input from First Nations, Inuit, and Métis. Its purpose is to provide a practical roadmap for Canada to effectively implement the principles and rights outlined in the UN Declaration, fostering lasting reconciliation through tangible actions taken in partnership with Indigenous Peoples.

  • The Action Plan is an evergreen document that was developed by the Department of Justice Canada in consultation and cooperation with Indigenous Peoples and other federal departments.
  • The Action Plan includes measures to address injustices, combat prejudice and eliminate all forms of violence, racism and discrimination against Indigenous Peoples.
  • The Action Plan promotes mutual respect and understanding as well as good relations, including through human rights education.
  • The Action Plan also includes measures related to monitoring, oversight, recourse or remedy and other accountability measures with respect to the implementation of the UN Declaration.
  • The Action Plan includes measures related to monitoring the implementation of the plan and reviewing and amending the plan.

The purpose of the Act is to affirm the UN Declaration as a universal international human rights instrument with application in Canadian law.

The Action Plan is a guide or roadmap to further advance reconciliation and achieve the objectives set out in the UN Declaration in a tangible way, in consultation with Indigenous Peoples.

On June 21, 2021, the Act became law and prompted Justice Canada to initiate a broad distinctions-based consultation and cooperation process with Indigenous Peoples. Over 120 written submissions proposing over 3,400 measures were received. This process aimed to help create the Action Plan and identify measures to ensure federal laws align with the UN Declaration. As a result of this collaborative effort with Indigenous Peoples, the Action Plan was unveiled on June 21, 2023.
